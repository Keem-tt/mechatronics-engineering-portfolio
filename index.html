OTO – Automated Whiteboard Cleaner

An open‑source, low‑cost solution that automates whiteboard cleaning using a modular rack‑and‑pinion drive, Arduino control and 3D‑printed parts.

Problem & Objective

In busy classrooms and collaboration spaces, thoroughly erasing large whiteboards can take up to two minutes. Over a week this lost time adds up to nearly an hour, disrupting instruction and reducing productivity. Instructors often rush the task, leaving ghosted markings that degrade surface quality and readability.

OTO was designed to solve this by automating the erase process. The goal is a portable device that traverses the board, wipes consistently and quietly, is simple to use, and costs under $150 including expansion.

Overview & Features

Low cost

The prototype BOM is about $136.87, with modular expansion costing roughly $3.73 per additional 0.5 ft of travel.

Modular & Scalable

The base prototype covers a 1.5 ft × 0.5 ft area; the rack‑and‑pinion rail can be extended to boards up to 8 ft wide by adding printed segments.

Simple Interface

A single push‑button starts a cleaning cycle. Built‑in calibration logic homes to dual limit switches and executes 10 sweeps per cycle.

Quiet & Efficient

Powered by a NEMA 17 stepper with a TMC2209 driver for silent microstepping and current control, OTO operates at less than 60 dB(A) while producing 2–4 N of eraser force.

Long Runtime

An 11.1 V 2200 mAh Li‑ion battery powers over 30 cleaning cycles on a single charge. A DC‑DC boost converter supplies 20 V for the motor while isolating logic.

Open & Repairable

All parts are designed in Fusion 360 and 3D‑printed in PLA+. The full CAD, code and report are open‑source so educators and makers can reproduce and iterate on the design.

System Architecture & Components

Subsystems

  • Cleaning Carriage: Houses the stepper motor, pinion gear, eraser pad and linear bearings for smooth travel.
  • Rack & Pinion Drive: Modular printed racks and a 16‑tooth printed pinion translate rotation to linear motion.
  • Control: Arduino Uno with TMC2209 driver via UART; dual normally open limit switches define home/end stops.
  • Power: 3‑cell Li‑ion battery with boost converter; toggle switch and low‑voltage cutoff for safety.

Key Components

  • NEMA 17 stepper (1.8°).
  • TMC2209 driver for quiet, precise motion.
  • Two normally open limit switches for homing.
  • 11.1 V 2200 mAh Li‑ion battery (≥30 cycles).
  • 8 mm aluminum linear rods with LM8UU bearings.
  • 3D‑printed rack segments and pinion gear in PLA+.
  • Foam + felt eraser pad affixed via Velcro.

Performance & Requirements

Cycle time

Each cleaning cycle comprises ten sweeps and completes in under two minutes.

Residual ink

After one cycle, less than 5% residual ink remains on the board.

Pad force

Eraser pad applies between 2–4 N of normal force for thorough wiping.

Noise & endurance

Operating noise stays below 60 dB(A) at one meter and the battery lasts for 30+ cycles.

Design Iteration

OTO underwent multiple iterations before reaching the current release:

  • Visual ideation: Early CAD focused on motion feasibility but lacked modularity.
  • Beta build: Introduced 0.5 ft rack segments and snap‑fit joints; validated modular rail concept.
  • Alpha build: Added power and communication bin and integrated carriage; highlighted issues with weight distribution and eraser glide.
  • Release candidate: Redesigned racks into three‑part plates, added adjustable Velcro‑mounted pads and optimized cable management.

Manufacturing & Materials

All mechanical parts are fabricated via Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) using grey PLA+ on an Elegoo Neptune 3 Pro. Prints use 0.2 mm layers, 3 walls, 4 top/bottom layers, 20% grid infill and 50 mm/s speed. Long components are divided into ≤0.5 ft sections and snap‑fit joints are tolerance‑tested in 0.1 mm increments for easy assembly.

Impact

By automating a mundane yet necessary task, OTO returns nearly an hour of instructional time each week per classroom. Consistent erasing extends board life and improves legibility. With a total cost under $150, OTO is accessible to underserved schools and DIY makers. The open‑source design encourages learning and community improvements.

Future Work

Planned improvements include sensorless homing to simplify wiring, enhanced wiping mechanisms for broader coverage, and pre‑wired harness kits with go/no‑go gauges for assembly quality assurance.
